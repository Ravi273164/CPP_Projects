A Tribute To Missile man Of India

Abul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam

1931-2015

Abul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam, popularly known as A.P.J. Abdul Kalam, was an eminent Indian scientist, visionary, and politician who served as the 11th President of India from 2002 to 2007. Born on October 15, 1931, in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, Kalam's life journey is a remarkable story of dedication, perseverance, and commitment to the betterment of his nation.

Kalam's early life was marked by humble beginnings. He hailed from a modest family, and his father, a boat owner, was not affluent. Despite financial constraints, Kalam exhibited an early curiosity and passion for learning. His educational journey began at the Schwartz Higher Secondary School, where he excelled in academics and showed a keen interest in science and mathematics.

In 1954, Kalam graduated in aeronautical engineering from the Madras Institute of Technology. His foray into the world of science and technology marked the commencement of a distinguished career. He joined the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) as a scientist, where he made significant contributions to India's military capabilities. Kalam's work on the development of indigenous missile systems, including the successful launch of the Agni and Prithvi missiles, earned him the moniker "Missile Man of India."

Kalam's commitment to advancing India's space capabilities led to his association with the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO). He played a pivotal role in various space missions, including the successful launch of the satellite Rohini. His expertise and leadership qualities eventually propelled him to the position of the Chief Scientific Adviser to the Prime Minister and the Secretary of the Defence Research and Development Organization.

Beyond his scientific endeavors, Kalam was a passionate advocate for education. He believed that education was the key to unlocking a nation's potential and empowering its citizens. Throughout his life, he engaged with students, encouraging them to dream big and pursue careers in science and technology. His book "Wings of Fire," an autobiographical account, resonated with readers, especially the youth, inspiring many to overcome challenges and aspire to greatness.

In 2002, A.P.J. Abdul Kalam's life took a historic turn when he became the President of India, succeeding K.R. Narayanan. His presidency was marked by a focus on education, youth, and national development. Kalam's unconventional approach to the presidency endeared him to the public, as he continued to connect with students and citizens across the country.

Known for his simple lifestyle and accessibility, Kalam's presidency left an indelible mark on the collective consciousness of the nation. He was not only a statesman but also a role model for millions. His speeches, often peppered with anecdotes from his own life, resonated with people from all walks of life.

Post his presidential tenure, Kalam continued to be actively involved in various educational and scientific initiatives. He remained committed to nurturing the next generation of scientists, engineers, and leaders. Tragically, on July 27, 2015, while delivering a lecture at the Indian Institute of Management Shillong, Kalam collapsed and passed away.

A.P.J. Abdul Kalam's legacy extends far beyond his impressive scientific contributions. He is remembered as a people's president, a visionary leader, and a source of inspiration for the youth. His teachings emphasize the importance of dreams, hard work, and dedication in achieving personal and national goals. In a world often marred by complexity, Kalam's life serves as a beacon of simplicity, integrity, and unwavering commitment to the betterment of humanity.
